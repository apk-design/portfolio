North Star Wellness Resources

A 24-hour design exercise for an interview brief: create a responsible, empathetic experience for people seeking mental health or substance-use resources. The challenge was to prioritize what truly matters when time and information are limited.

Healthcare UX Branding

Research

Though I did not get many resources for this project, I was very kindly given 4 comparison sites for this field. So naturally, I began with what I know - doing a qualitative analysis!

Approach

Looking for help with mental health or substance use is rarely neutral. It usually comes with fear, hesitation, and a lot of social baggage. As I reviewed existing treatment websites, I noticed a pattern of bright visuals, urgency-heavy copy, and constant pressure to ‚Äúact now.‚Äù

That approach might work for conversions, but it can also make an already stressful moment feel louder and more overwhelming. I wanted to see what it would look like to do the opposite.

Branding, Visual Direction, and the Birth of North Star Wellness Resources

While brainstorming for this project, I was between a few different names. Phoenix, Kintsugi, Anchor, Lighthouse - with the implication being changing your life or providing stability if you're knee deep in chaos.

I ended up settling on 'North Star', as the concept and function of the titular term is built around orientation, not transformation. The brand doesn‚Äôt promise clarity or change. It‚Äôs meant to feel like a steady reference point for people who aren‚Äôt sure what their next step is yet. I chose Wellness Resources as it had the propensity to cover a lot of different problem areas.

That idea shaped everything from the name and logo to the overall visual language. I kept the branding calm, aiming for something that felt approachable without being performative.

Dark Mode, my Personal ‚òÜ North Star ‚òÜ

The site uses a predominantly dark interface on purpose. For a lot of people, especially those dealing with stigma or fear around needing help, visibility itself can feel uncomfortable.

The darker UI:

  • Feels more private and contained
  • Works better for late-night or discreet browsing

This was as much of an aesthetic choice as much as an emotional one. The goal was to lower friction and make it easier for someone to stay on the site long enough to actually absorb the information. I did want to ensure that for those who were browsing in front of others, that it remained an intimate and optimistic experience.

The 24-hour Outcome

Within the 24-hour constraint, this concept focuses on emotional safety, clear hierarchy, and user autonomy. Though this is not a real site, even in an imaginary context, I would rather help a vulnerable individual feel oriented enough to decide what they want to do next, when they‚Äôre ready. That being said, I ran into some issues while on this project, that I will go into in the next section.

Technical Difficulties + My Mistakes

Or, the tale of how I put too many eggs in a single basket, and how sometimes, the basket is me. Weird analogy, but I promise it will make sense if you read further.

Figma Makes Me Eat My Words

My first mistake was to rest far too much on Figma as my main graphics program. While I do think I am pretty talented in Figma, there are two major things I would typically not use it for - those being graphic / logo creation, and typesetting.

Arguably, the most important thing I was not aware of whatsoever is that Figma will start to slow down if you use more than 2GB on a free plan. For some context, I have created branding and UX + UI flows for a start up, very literally ~1000 screens of work for local companies, not to mention all the various kanban boards, personal sprints, art ideas, and so much more content on my free figma plan.

Foolishly, even as the program got slower, I carried on, knowing that a crash was coming, just not knowing when. And then it did, 4 consecutive times. And then again, to the point where I was having to cmd + opt + esc Figma every 15 minutes or so. Pasting a group took a whopping 30 seconds. In my own hubris to use the program to the fullest, I ended up impairing its usefulness entirely.
