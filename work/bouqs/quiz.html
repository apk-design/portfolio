  • Role: Product designer (research, UX, visual design).

Problem

Wedding customers often arrived with a vague idea like ‚Äúromantic but not too traditional‚Äù and struggled to translate that into filters and SKUs. Solution Overview

I designed a 10-step, image-driven quiz that feels like building a Pinterest moodboard. Each step captures a specific decision factor that feeds into a recommendation engine, which surfaces three curated collections tuned to the couple‚Äôs style, logistics, and budget.

What the Quiz Measures

Each step is intentionally visual and quick, but maps to a concrete set of attributes for the recommendation logic:

1. Wedding Style

First, couples choose their overall wedding style from options like Modern, Classic, Boho, Romantic, Vintage. Multiple selection is allowed so the quiz can support blended aesthetics.

4. Favorite Flowers

A multi-select gallery lets users highlight flowers they love (e.g., roses, hydrangeas, ranunculus). These are treated as boosts in the recommendation logic, raising matching designs to the top.

The quiz then shows a grid of wedding-day photos and asks users to ‚ÄúSelect the images that resonate with you the most.‚Äù This captures softer attributes like lighting, texture, and composition that don‚Äôt fit neatly into a drop-down.

7. Budget Tier

Personalized Results

After the quiz, couples land on a tailored results page with three curated collections. Each collection is pre-filtered by style, palette, and budget, and includes the right mix of bouquets, boutonnieres, and centerpieces for the specified bridal party and guest count.

UX & Accessibility Considerations

  • Each step has a clear progress indicator on the left so users always know where they are and can move backward.
  • Instructions like ‚ÄúSelect all that apply‚Äù vs. ‚ÄúSelect one‚Äù are explicit and repeated near the CTA.

  • The quiz can be exited at any time; partial answers still improve default recommendations.

Outcomes & Reflection

The quiz reframes wedding shopping as a guided, emotionally resonant experience: couples express who they are, and The Bouqs translates that into concrete product recommendations. Even without implementation data, the design explores how lightweight, image-led questions can dramatically reduce choice overload and make large wedding catalogs feel curated and personal.
